About the Temple

Udupi Krishna Temple Heritage

The Udupi Krishna Temple, lovingly called Shri Krishna Matha, is the beating heart of Vaishnavite devotion in South India. Founded in the 13th century by Jagadguru Sri Madhvacharya, it is dedicated to Lord Krishna in his child form.

The sanctum’s famed Kanakana Kindi — a silver-plated lattice window — was created after saint Kanakadasa was denied entry due to caste barriers. Lord Krishna is said to have turned westward so Kanakadasa could receive darshan through this window, and to this day every devotee first prays through that portal.

Eight monasteries, the Ashta Mathas, still manage the temple in a biennial rotation called Paryaya. Their seers oversee the daily Maha Prasada, feed thousands, and preserve the Dravidian–Hoysala architectural blend that defines the Matha precincts.

Prime Minister’s Visit

The Prime Minister returns to the Math after sixteen years to lead the concluding chant of the Bhagavad Gita, inaugurate the Kanaka Kavacha, and spotlight the Math’s Vaishnavite legacy for the nation.

Festival Context

The Laksha Kantha Geetha Parayana is part of the Sri Sugunendra Theertha Swamiji’s Vishwagita Paryaya, comprising Sant Sangama, Bhajanotsava, Sant Sandesha, Sant Sanmana, and immersive Gita workshops.

Sacred Situation

Where History Meets Living Tradition

Every ritual during Laksha Kantha Geetha Parayana happens inside the sanctum of Sri Krishna Math, where Sri Madhwacharya himself installed the Bala Krishna idol he discovered at Malpe seashore.

Madhwacharya’s Discovery

The Acharya lifted the mud-encrusted idol from a block of gopichandana clay, carried it to Udupi, and enshrined it facing west so that devotees could have darshan through the famous Kanakana Kindi.

Seers & Sevaks

The eight Maths take turns (Paryaya) to perform seva. During this Laksha Geetha observance, Sri Sugunendra Theertha Swamiji and Sri Vidhyadheesha Theertha Swamiji personally orchestrate the parayana.

Kanaka Mantapa

The new golden mantapa and Kanaka Kavacha provide a resplendent backdrop for the Geetha chanting, with thousands queueing along the Car Street and Madhwa Sarovara to witness the alankaras.
